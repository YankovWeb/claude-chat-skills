# Negotiation War Room

Negotiations are won in preparation and lost in improvisation. This skill produces a one-page **battle card** the user can glance at mid-conversation, plus scripted openings — because the person who has pre-decided their moves cannot be rushed into bad ones.

## Intelligence gathering (from context or brief questions)
What's being negotiated · who's across the table and what pressure THEY are under · the user's real alternatives · any numbers already spoken aloud (spoken numbers are anchors already working).

## The battle card

```
🎯 TARGET: the ambitious-but-defensible outcome (not the "happy" one — the one
   requiring a slightly uncomfortable ask)
🛡️ BATNA: best alternative if talks die + its honest value. Weak BATNA? The #1
   prep task is improving it BEFORE the talk (one parallel option changes everything)
🚪 WALK-AWAY: the number/terms where leaving beats staying. Decided now, in calm.
⚓ ANCHOR: who states a number first and what it is. Anchor when you know the
   range (aim just outside their comfort, inside plausibility); invite their
   number when you don't. Include the anchor sentence verbatim.
🪜 CONCESSION LADDER: 3 pre-planned steps of SHRINKING size (e.g. −8% → −3% → −1%;
   shrinking steps signal a floor approaching). Every step trades for something —
   "if you can do X, I can do Y." Naked concessions teach them to keep pushing.
🎁 TRADEABLES: 4–6 things cheap for the user but valuable to them, and vice versa
   (timing, scope, exclusivity, payment terms, testimonials, risk). Deals are made
   here, not on the headline number.
🧠 THEIR SIDE: their BATNA, their deadline, what their boss measures them on, and
   the story they need to tell internally to say yes. Give them that story.
```

## Scripts (verbatim, in the user's language and voice)
1. **Opening frame** — sets collaborative-but-firm tone
2. **The ask/anchor** — then the hardest instruction in negotiation: *stop talking*
3. **Response to their likely pushback** — the top 2 objections, answered
4. **Flinch response** — for an aggressive lowball: calm, non-counter, question-based ("Help me understand how you arrived there")
5. **The pause line** — "I'll need to consider this and come back tomorrow" (pre-scripted so it's available under pressure; almost nothing must be answered live)

## Table rules to include on the card
- Silence after numbers. Whoever fills the silence pays for it.
- Questions beat statements: "What flexibility do you have?" > "That's too low."
- Never bid against yourself — no new number until they've responded to the last one.
- Conditional everything: every "yes" wears an "if".
- Log each round; drift from the plan is how good BATNAs lose to bad deals.

## Live mode
If the user returns mid-negotiation ("they countered with X"), skip theory: assess the counter against the card in 2 lines, then give the exact next message/line to send. Update the card if the situation moved.
