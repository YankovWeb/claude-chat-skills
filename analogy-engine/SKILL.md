# Analogy Engine

A metaphor decorates; an analogy *transfers structure*. The difference is discipline: a real analogy has an explicit mapping between parts, and an honest boundary where the mapping fails. An analogy without a declared breaking point is a future misconception with good PR.

## Process

### 1. Decompose the target
Before hunting for analogies, extract the concept's **relational skeleton** — 3–5 relationships that make it what it is (not surface features). E.g., for "technical debt": (a) a shortcut yields speed now, (b) cost accrues over time, (c) accrual compounds, (d) you can pay principal or keep paying interest.

### 2. Generate 3 candidate analogies from DIFFERENT source domains
Force diversity — pick from at least three of: household/kitchen, sports/games, biology/nature, money/trade, traffic/logistics, relationships/social, construction/craft. Same-domain candidates are redundant.

For the audience given, prefer source domains *they* have physical intuition for. Ask yourself: has this audience actually experienced the source? (Don't explain databases via baseball to a European audience.)

### 3. Load-test each candidate
Score each on:
- **Coverage** — how many skeleton relations does it map? (map count / total)
- **Slack** — how many *extra* implications does it smuggle in that are false for the target? (fewer = better; this is the danger metric)
- **Familiarity** — will this audience feel the source in their gut?

Pick the winner. Briefly say why the losers lost — that reasoning is reusable.

### 4. Deliver the winner in three parts

**a) The analogy itself** — 2–4 sentences, vivid, present tense.

**b) The mapping table** — explicit, this is what makes it rigorous:
| In the analogy | In reality |
|---|---|
| ... | ... |

**c) 🧨 Where it breaks** — 2–3 specific points where the analogy misleads, each with the correction. Frame as "the analogy suggests ___, but actually ___." Teaching the break is teaching the concept's real edges.

### 5. Optional extensions (offer, don't force)
- **Extension move**: one way the analogy can stretch to explain an *advanced* aspect (great for talks — "and here's the beautiful part…").
- **One-liner version**: the analogy compressed to a single quotable sentence for slides.

## Rules
- Never deliver an analogy without its break points. Non-negotiable.
- If the concept genuinely resists analogy (some do — e.g., quantum superposition famously breaks every classical source), say so and offer a *negative analogy* instead: "it is NOT like X in exactly this way," which is itself instructive.
- One great analogy beats three decent ones; deliver one, keep the runners-up to a single line each.
